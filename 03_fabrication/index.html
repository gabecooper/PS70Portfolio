Week 3: Hand Tools and Fabrication

Kinetic Sculpture

For my Kinetic sculpture, I wanted to make a marble conveyor belt. This ended up taking way, way more time than I expected. The easiest step was following the tutorial on the PHYSCI70 website to create housing for the motor. I ended up using the front pieces as a means of attaching the motor to the knob on my conveyor belt.

One challenge I had was selecting what material to use for the conveyor belt. In the end, like with real conveyor belts, I choose to use rubber. And, according to thistothat.com, I used Barge as an adhesive when I had to connect two ends of rubber together so that the length of my band was long enough. Likewise, I used Barge to attach wooden dowels to the rubber to hold the marbles as the motors turn. Another thing that was exciting to me was the idea that the system be closed. Specifically, that the marble gets returned from the top of the conveyor belt to the bottom. For that, I made a paper roller coaster. To attach the motor, I fitted it first with the cardboard piece from the tutorial and glued the cardboard piece to the wood dowel. The other dowel, I decided would be allow to spin freely. However, the first time I ran the machine, the motor was spinning really fast and broke the conveyor belt. Hence, for my final version, I decided to fit the motor with a gear and the spinning dowel with another gear (forming a 3:1 ratio) such that the dowel would spin a lot slower then the motor with more power. This aligns more with my goal of keeping the conveyor belt steady but functional. I also choose to redesign the entire project to make it take less materials, given that this is a prototype. I did use the SpurGear script to make the gear (24 teeth to 8).

5 / 17 / 26 - Updated!

While I was waiting for the CNC machine to free up for my final project, I chose to experiment with designing a geneva mechanism to estimate the speed of my DC motor under varying levels of voltage. It works by turning the clock disk 4 times a loop which is useful to me because it takes a continuous variable and makes it discrete which is easier for me to use when doing math to see how motor speed varies with voltage.

I laser cut all the pieces, and taped them together, and then used the voltage power box to power the motor. Admittedly, there were problems that came with having no baseplate and relying completely on duck tape and those sacrifices were primarily reliability.
